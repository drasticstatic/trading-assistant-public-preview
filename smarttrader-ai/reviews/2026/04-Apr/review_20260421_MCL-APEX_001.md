## 🧠 Behavioral Notes

**Pattern 7 (SL modification) — active:** TP and SL moved during the trade. With 18 minutes to the 4 PM close and the prop firm hard stop at 16:58, there was minimal time for structural SL placement. The result: a variable, unanchored stop that left $107 on the table (MFE $248 vs exit $141) and required emotional management in lieu of mechanical rules.

**Pattern 8 (exit passivity) — partial improvement:** This is the first profitable MCL trade with an active exit. Christopher did pull the trigger and exit in profit at 89.70. However, the exit was $0.85 above the MFE continuation (87.85), and exit efficiency was 43.25% — over half the available move was left behind. This is real progress from the Mar 26 MCL and BTCC losses, but the gap between "took profit" and "optimized exit" remains wide.

**"Stable before entering but not while in the trade":** The adverse move to 92.23 (-$112) before reversal would have been emotionally stressful without a firm SL anchor. This describes the emotional experience: conviction dissolved once price moved against the position. The trade rescued itself — price reversed and produced profit — rather than the exit being actively managed from a calm state.

**"Chasing price, FOMO, not in plan, trading against the trend":** These five self-identified mistakes matter. The setup was ZTH Pivot — a countertrend reversal. Energy futures were trending. The last-minute formulation across multiple index charts is a tell for a thesis assembled post-hoc rather than a plan-consistent setup.

**"Almost exited in the red":** The fact that this crossed Christopher's mind during the MAE move confirms the emotional state. This exit was reactive to P&L state (I'm in the red → I should exit), not structural (price reached X → exit). The outcome is positive, but the decision process was not.

**What went right:** 1 micro contract. Correct instrument sizing for an unplanned scalp. The observation that indices showed resting liquidity aligned with the ZTH pivot sell idea — that read was correct even if the execution chain was not.

## 📝 Notes for Coaches

> *"It was a last minute idea with many orders I planned on each indices charts, I almost exited in the red but wow the market quickly secured profit as I moved TP into a place I hoped to at least see a green day for a change :-)"*

The emotional honesty here is valuable: this was a trade born from wanting a green day, not from a plan. The "many orders planned on each indices charts" language describes a wide-net scan for any entry that might produce a win — ZTH Pivot was applied, but applied after the impulse to trade, not before it.

The MAE move to 92.23 was 1.12 points adverse before reversal. At $100/point that's -$112 on a trade that had no firm SL. "Accepted risk with variable SL as market was soon closing" is a rationalization that happens to have worked here. The prop firm closes positions at 16:58 — the implicit risk ceiling is the open P&L at that time, not a structural level.

The exit efficiency story: best available exit was 87.85 at 16:10, just 1 minute after Christopher exited at 16:09 at 89.70. This is not passivity in the traditional sense — he exited actively. But the move continued immediately after exit, suggesting the exit was driven by P&L anxiety ("I have profit, take it before it disappears") rather than a level or signal.

**The growth edge here is the gap between "I traded and won" and "I executed a plan."** Both matter. Right now Christopher is developing the habit of being in winning trades. The next step is pre-defining when to exit before entering — even a simple rule like "I exit at prior session low" or "I trail with each 0.5-point move in my favor" converts emotional management into structural management.
